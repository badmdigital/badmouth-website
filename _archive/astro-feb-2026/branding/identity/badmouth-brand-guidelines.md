## 6. TONE OF VOICE

**Brand Vibe:** Bold. Real. Psychology-driven. A little salty, always smart.

### Voice Traits

| Trait | How We Use It |
|---|---|
| **Bold** | We say what others won't. No fluff, no fence-sitting. |
| **No-BS** | We skip the fancy talk and cut straight to what matters. |
| **Strategic** | Every line educates, motivates, or converts. Clarity over cleverness. |
| **Witty** | A smart jab or sharp analogy, but only when it serves the point. |
| **Helpful Coach** | We've been in the trenches. We teach. We guide. We don't lecture. |
| **Real** | Light swearing for impact and punch. Never just to be edgy. Think: "hell," "damn," "sh*t" (sparingly). |

### Writing Rules

- Write at a 7th-grade reading level or lower
- Use contractions. Write how people talk
- If it sounds like a LinkedIn influencer wrote it, delete it
- Kill corporate speak. No "utilize," "subsequently," "innovative solutions," or "cutting-edge"
- Give real advice. Steps, stories, and simple examples
- Swear with purpose. Don't overdo it
- No em dashes between words
- Exclamation points are rare. You're not hosting a giveaway

### Quick Voice Test

Before you publish, ask:

- Would a 13-year-old understand this?
- Is this short and sharp?
- Does it feel like something I'd actually say?
- Did I cut the filler and say the thing?

## 11. VISUAL IDENTITY

### The Aesthetic

**"Digital Grunge" meets "90s Analog Tech."**

Think VHS tracking errors, cassette tapes, photocopied zines, and early computing interfaces. The visual identity is the antithesis of clean, sterile corporate design. It's raw, loud, and unapologetically analog in a digital world.

### Key Visual Principles

- **Scanlines and noise.** Everything should feel like it's being viewed on a dying CRT monitor
- **Glitch effects.** Text separation (red/cyan), clipping masks, sudden shifts
- **Hard edges.** No soft shadows or rounded corners. Thick, brutal borders
- **Pixelation.** Icons and cursors should feel 1-bit or 8-bit
- **High contrast is non-negotiable.** Black is the canvas. Red is the alarm

## 12. COLOR PALETTE

| Color Name | Hex Code | Usage |
|---|---|---|
| **Bad Black** | `#0a0a0a` | Primary background, text on light |
| **Bad Red** | `#ff0000` | Primary accent, warnings, glitch layers |
| **Bad Teal** | `#4FD1C5` | Secondary accent, glitch layers, "cool" tech elements |
| **Bad White** | `#f0f0f0` | Text on dark, paper backgrounds |
| **Bad Gray** | `#333333` | Secondary backgrounds, UI elements |
| **Warning Yellow** | `#FACC15` | Warning labels, stickers, callouts |

### Color Rules

- Bad Black + Bad White is the foundation of everything
- Bad Red is the alarm. Use it for primary accents, CTAs, and emphasis. Don't flood layouts with it
- Bad Teal is the counterbalance. Use it to signal tech, data, or secondary elements
- Warning Yellow is for label moments: stickers, warnings, callouts. Sparingly
- Never use pastels, gradients, or soft tones. If it looks like a SaaS landing page, start over

## 13. TYPOGRAPHY

### Display / Headings

**Font:** `Anton` or `Oswald`

- Usage: Hero titles, section headers, "loud" statements
- Style: Uppercase, tight tracking (`tracking-tighter`), massive size
- Effect: Often paired with glitch effects or outline strokes
- If it doesn't feel like it's yelling at you from a concert poster, it's too quiet

### Body / Data

**Font:** `JetBrains Mono`

- Usage: Paragraph text, UI labels, metadata, dates
- Style: Monospaced, raw, technical
- Vibe: "Terminal code" or "Typewriter"
- Reads like system output. That's the point

### Type Rules

- Headings are always uppercase
- Body copy is sentence case
- Minimum heading size on web: 48px for hero, 32px for sections
- Never use decorative or script fonts
- Letter spacing on headings should be tight, not loose
- If it looks like a PowerPoint template, it's wrong

## 14. LOGO USAGE

### Primary Mark

The Badmouth Digital logo is the brand's most recognizable asset. Treat it with discipline.

### Clear Space

Maintain clear space around the logo equal to the height of the "B" in Badmouth on all sides. No text, graphics, or other elements should encroach on this space.

### Minimum Size

- Digital: 120px wide minimum
- Print: 1 inch wide minimum

### Acceptable Versions

- **Primary:** White logo on Bad Black background
- **Inverted:** Bad Black logo on light backgrounds
- **Accent:** Bad Red logo for special applications (merch, event branding)

### Logo Don'ts

- Don't stretch, skew, or rotate the logo
- Don't apply drop shadows, bevels, or 3D effects
- Don't place the logo on busy or low-contrast backgrounds
- Don't change the logo colors outside approved versions
- Don't add taglines, icons, or decorations to the logo itself
- Don't recreate the logo in a different font
- Don't shrink it below minimum size

## 15. PHOTOGRAPHY AND IMAGERY DIRECTION

### What We Use

- Real photos of real work. Job sites, crews, tools, trucks, finished projects
- Gritty, honest, not over-produced. Natural light over studio setups
- Screenshots and screen recordings of actual systems, dashboards, and results
- Before/after comparisons when they tell a real story

### What We Avoid

- Stock photos of people shaking hands in conference rooms
- "Corporate Memphis" style illustrations (flat, faceless, pastel characters)
- Overly polished lifestyle photography that feels disconnected from our audience
- AI-generated imagery that looks fake or generic
- Clip art or dated web graphics

### Treatment

- Photos can be treated with grain, scanline overlays, or slight desaturation to match the brand aesthetic
- When using imagery on dark backgrounds, a subtle noise texture overlay helps unify the visual
- Avoid heavy filters that make images unrecognizable

### The Test

If the image could appear on any agency's website, it's wrong. Our imagery should feel like it belongs in the same world as our copy: direct, real, and built for people who work with their hands.

2. **Instagram** — Visual proof, reels, before/after
3. **YouTube** — Long-form education, walkthroughs, proof
4. **LinkedIn** — Thought leadership and B2B credibility (secondary)

### Content Pillars

- **Proof:** Results, case studies, screenshots, client wins
- **Education:** Tips, frameworks, how-it-works breakdowns
- **Call-outs:** Industry BS, bad marketing practices, myths debunked
- **Behind the scenes:** How we build, what we're working on, real process

### Social Voice

- Insightful, a little spicy
- Mix proof, punchlines, and real talk
- Posts should feel like something a sharp friend would text you, not something a brand posted
- No hashtag stuffing. Use 3-5 relevant hashtags max on platforms that benefit from them

### Visual Standards for Social

- Use brand colors and typography in all designed posts
- Maintain the "digital grunge" aesthetic even in quick posts
- Video thumbnails should use bold text overlays (Anton/Oswald, uppercase)
- No Canva templates that look like every other small business page

### Social Don'ts

- Don't post motivational quotes on sunset backgrounds
- Don't use engagement bait ("Tag someone who needs this!")
- Don't chase trends that don't connect to our message
- Don't post just to post. Every piece should educate, prove, or challenge

## 17. EMAIL GUIDELINES

### Voice

Conversational and confident. Like a sharp friend who's in your corner. Emails should feel like they came from a real person, not a marketing department.

### Structure

- Subject lines: Short, direct, curiosity-driven. No clickbait
- Opening line: Hook them in one sentence. No "hope this finds you well"
- Body: Short paragraphs. One idea per paragraph. 2-3 sentences max per block
- CTA: One clear action per email. Don't split attention

### Formatting

- Plain text style preferred. Light formatting only
- Bold sparingly for emphasis
- No heavy HTML templates that look like newsletters from 2014
- Images only when they serve a purpose (screenshots, results, proof)
- Always preview on mobile first. Mike reads email on his phone between jobs

### Email Don'ts

- Don't open with the company name or a greeting longer than one line
- Don't use multiple CTAs competing for attention
- Don't send emails that read like blog posts. Keep it tight
- Don't use "Dear valued customer" or any variation of corporate greeting

## 18. COPYWRITING STRUCTURE (LABELING SYSTEM)

All website copy follows this labeling convention:

| Element | Purpose |
|---|---|
| **Eyebrow** | Small text above the headline. Sets context or category |
| **Headline** | The main statement. Bold, direct, scroll-stopping |
| **Subhead** | Supports the headline. Adds clarity or a second angle |
| **Body Copy** | The substance. Short paragraphs, plain language, real talk |
| **CTA** | Clear action with link text and destination |

### Copy Rules

- Never use "this page is for" language
- No em dashes between words
- Choppy, punchy rhythm. Short sentences. Active verbs
- Aggressive metaphors are welcome when they serve the point
- Every section should be able to stand alone and still make sense

---

## 19. FORMATTING AND STYLE

- Use **bold** for emphasis or headers
- Use lists and bullets to break up information (in docs and web, not in prose)
- CAPS can be used sparingly for punch (not like YOU'RE YELLING)
- Exclamation points are rare
- Emojis are acceptable when they add clarity or tone, never decorative
- Avoid hyphen overload. Use periods, commas, or dashes where natural

## 20. THE DO'S AND DON'TS (MASTER LIST)

### Do

- Write like a human who gives a damn
- Use plain language a 13-year-old could understand
- Lead with the problem before offering the solution
- Show proof. Screenshots, numbers, real client results
- Speak to Mike. Always. If it wouldn't resonate with a contractor between jobs, rethink it
- Use the Start With Them™ framework as the foundation for all messaging
- Build trust before the pitch
- Test everything on mobile first
- Keep it short. Then make it shorter

### Don't

- Sound like every other agency
- Use buzzwords, jargon, or corporate filler
- Chase trends or recommend tactics just because they're popular
- Over-design. If it looks like a SaaS startup landing page, start over
- Use stock photos of handshakes or people pointing at whiteboards
- Write copy that talks about us before it talks about them
- Promise what we can't deliver
- Use AI-generated content without heavy human editing
- Forget that every word, image, and design choice either builds trust or breaks it
